TCM Liver Treatment Methods

In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), the Liver plays a crucial role in regulating the flow of Qi, storing Blood, and managing emotions. When Liver function is compromised, TCM primarily approaches treatment in four ways: Dispersing, Soothing, Supplementing, and Subduing Ascendant Yang (Qian Yang).

Key Herb Comparison

Herb Property Taste Core Efficacy
Chai Hu Cool Bitter, Acrid Harmonizes & reduces fever, soothes Liver & resolves depression
Xiang Fu Zi Neutral or slightly cool Acrid, Bitter, Sweet Moves Qi & resolves depression, regulates menstruation & stops pain
Bai Shao Yao Cool Sour, Bitter Nourishes Blood & astringes Yin, relaxes tension & stops pain
Gou Teng Slightly Cool Salty Subdues Yang, clears Heat & extinguishes Wind

The Four Treatment Methods

1. Dispersing: Scattering Liver Heat with Light Qi - Chai Hu

When a pathogenic influence, especially Heat, enters the Liver, it can get stuck in a 'Half-Exterior, Half-Interior' state, neither fully external nor internal. This manifests as alternating chills and fever, a bitter taste in the mouth, and tightness in the flanks.

The primary herb used for this is Chai Hu. Chai Hu is light and has an ascending quality, allowing it to gently lift and scatter the stagnant heat from the Liver out of the body. This is called a 'Harmonizing' action, like mediating a conflict.

Core Efficacy

  • Harmonizes & reduces fever
  • Soothes the Liver & resolves depression

Key Symptoms

  • Alternating chills and fever
  • Fullness in the chest/flanks
  • Bitter taste in mouth

2. Soothing: Powerfully Unblocking Qi Stagnation - Xiang Fu Zi

Excessive stress or suppressed emotions can cause the Liver Qi (è‚æ°£) to stagnate. This is called 'Liver Qi Stagnation'. As the flow of Qi is blocked, it can cause chest tightness, flank pain, poor digestion, and in women, menstrual pain or irregularities.

The primary herb used for this is Xiang Fu Zi. Xiang Fu Zi is known as the 'Commander-in-Chief of Qi disorders' due to its outstanding ability to unblock stagnant Qi. Its acrid and aromatic nature strongly moves and circulates the stagnant Qi throughout the body.

Core Efficacy

  • Moves Qi & resolves depression
  • Regulates menstruation & stops pain

  • Distending pain in chest/flanks
  • Abdominal distention
  • Irregular menstruation & pain

3. Supplementing: Softening a Dry Liver by Replenishing Blood - Bai Shao Yao

The Liver is the 'storehouse of Blood'. When the Liver Blood is sufficient, our muscles move smoothly, our eyes are clear, and our emotions are stable. If Liver Blood becomes deficient from overwork or poor nutrition, the Liver becomes stiff, like a dry sponge.

The primary herb used for this is Bai Shao Yao. Bai Shao Yao is a primary Blood-supplementing herb that also adds Yin fluids. It replenishes the deficient Liver Blood, making the Liver soft and moist. It relaxes stiff muscles and calms the unstable Liver Qi, which can rise up.

Core Efficacy

  • Nourishes Blood & astringes Yin
  • Relaxes tension & stops pain

Key Symptoms

  • Flank pain
  • Dizziness, dry eyes

4. Subduing Ascendant Yang (Qian Yang): Calming Liver Yang Rising - Gou Teng

When Liver Yin and Blood (Yin Xue) are insufficient to restrain the Yang Qi, the condition of 'Liver Yang Rising (Gan Yang Shang Kang)' develops. This causes Yang Qi to rush upwards, leading to symptoms like headaches, dizziness, and facial flushing. If severe, it can evolve into 'Internal Liver Wind (Gan Feng Nei Dong)', causing convulsions and tremors.

Gou Teng is a key herb used to subdue (Qian Yang) this rapidly ascending Liver Yang, bringing calm and stability.

Core Efficacy

  • Ping Gan Qian Yang: Subdues the ascending Liver Yang Qi.
  • Ping Gan Xi Feng: Calms the Liver and extinguishes Internal Wind.
  • Qing Re Zhen Jing: Clears Heat and calms convulsions or fright.

Key Symptoms

  • Headache, dizziness (often hypertension-related)
  • Convulsions, tremors of hands and feet, seizures (especially in children)
  • Insomnia, restlessness

Clinical Applications & Modern Research

  • Usage: "The common dosage is 6-15g. Its efficacy is reduced by prolonged decoction, so it is crucial to add it late (Hou Xia) and decoct for only about 15-20 minutes."
  • Modern Research: "Actions such as lowering blood pressure, sedation, anti-convulsant, and vasodilatory effects have been reported."
