Yin Yang Diagram

When we look at the yin-yang diagram, it is not simply divided into black and white halves. It resembles fish chasing each other's tails. It is very dynamic. This depicts the continuous movement between yin and yang. Moreover, there is a white dot in the black area and a black dot in the white area. Additionally, one side is thick while the other is thin like a tail, so there are places where yin and yang are completely equal (horizontal center), and parts where one side dominates while the other is barely present (vertical center). Although yin and yang are divided, their proportions differ. Why was this simple yin-yang diagram drawn in such a way? This diagram addresses all the important properties of yin and yang.

1. Why does it move?

Yin and yang are not fixed. The shaded part of a hill in the morning receives sunlight in the afternoon. Yin becomes yang. The same applies to our bodies and diseases. Just as everything in nature changes through the four seasons, yin and yang transform. This is called the mutual transformation of yin and yang.

2. What do the white dot in the black area and the black dot in the white area represent?

Yin and yang are not a dichotomy. There is no purely yin or purely yang. There is always yang within yin, and yin within yang. The same applies to constitution and disease. There are no purely heat diseases or purely cold diseases. This is called the infinite divisibility of yin and yang.

3. What is the meaning of the different area proportions?

While yin and yang are divided, some areas have more yin and others have more yang. However, even when the proportions differ, the total sum remains the same. This expresses how yin and yang consume each other. When one increases, the other naturally decreases. And when this tendency becomes extreme, it returns to the original state. It moves like a seesaw. When one rises, the other falls, but neither rises nor falls eternally—they trace a circle. In our bodies too, when yin is deficient, yang rises, and when yang rises, yin falls. When fever occurs, body fluids are damaged, and when blood is deficient, deficiency heat arises. This is called the mutual consumption of yin and yang.

*Modern Traditional Chinese Medicine tends to ignore this movement, proportional changes, and non-binary nature, and instead tries to categorize diseases and people in a simplistic binary way as either yin or yang, cold or hot—this represents a fundamental misunderstanding of yin and yang.*
